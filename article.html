Watch the Damn Films!

It used to be the case that one had to cross many miles, even continents and oceans, to gain access to the world’s cultural assets - these invaluable works in the fields of art, literature and philosophy.

We have come a long way since. In the case of classic films, our particular field of interest, not only are they becoming increasingly available online, but a large number of them are considered as belonging to the public domain.

In light of this one might wonder: where does the value of these films lie for the modern viewer? How could one get to know them? Is it by storing and archiving them? By reading, writing and referencing them? Through courses and lectures?

While these are all valuable approaches, at the core of it there is a simpler answer: to benefit from cinema one needs to experience cinema, and to understand cinema history one needs to experience cinema history. That is, actually watch the films!

La Filmothèque, our personal project, was built with that in mind: a platform to learn about classic films through experience. We are putting together and organizing these wonderful films – well known and obscure, Hollywood classics and world cinema, mainstream and avant-garde, silent and talkies – with the hope to expose new audience to films, genres and directors they might not have been exposed to otherwise.

We have assembled together a few notable examples belonging to the public domain, showing some of the fascinating themes one might come across.

The Cure is Chaplin’s tenth short comedy with Mutual Film Corporation. It was written, directed, and starring Chaplin himself, and is probably one of the funniest Chaplin films of all time.

Unlike Chaplin’s later films, which tended to have a strong sentimental side, The Cure does not revolve around subjects like poverty, harsh childhood, or unreturned love, but provides laughs from start to finish.

It is also interesting that Chaplin does not appear as the usual iconic “Little Tramp”, but as a wealthy alcoholic bourgeois. Drunken characters is an act Chaplin had specialized in from back in his days at the British Music Hall.

The story begins when Chaplin’s character arrives at a health spa, supposedly to overcome his alcoholism. It is soon clear however that he has no intention of doing that, as his suitcase is full of booze. One thing leads to another, and at some point everyone in the health spa gets completely drunk and things get wild.

The Lodger is Hitchcock’s third film as a director and one of his most notable silent films. This films sees Hitchcock’s cinematic language coming to maturity, and introduces themes such as murder, mystery and suspense which he later became identified with. Hitchcock himself considered The Lodger to be his first true film.

The film is loosely based on Jack the Ripper, and drives inspiration from a novel by Mrs. Belloc Lowndes.

It is situated in London, where a serial killer called “The Avenger” takes blond-haired girls as his victims. Around the same time, Mr. and Mrs. Bunting take in a mysterious lodger. As the man and their blond-haired daughter grow close, suspicions begin to arise.

Pandora’s Box, directed by G.W. Pabst, is the film that made Louise Brooks a star and a remarkable icon of the Jazz age, and of modernity.

The film is based on several different plays by Frank Wedekind, an important early expressionist dramatist. It tells the tragic story of Lulu, a good hearted prostitute whose life lead her into cruel circumstances and a fatal fate.

Pabst’s direction, unlike any other silent German Expressionist film, is naturalistic and strikingly modern.

Earth is a Soviet film by Ukrainian director Alexander Dovzhenko - a cinematic poet and one of Andrei Tarkovsky’s most admired directors. This is the third film in his “Ukrain” trilogy, alongside Zvenigora and Arsenal.

As a film-poem, the surface plot in Earth not the main emphasis. The film shines with its rich montage, depicting the cycle of life and death, growth and decay, and the “earth” itself.

Andrei Tarkovsky had said about Earth that he used to watch is over and over again, and would re-watch it before he began working on each of his films.

To learn more, watch the films!

This is just a taste of what classic cinema has to offer. These films and many others are available online and on La Filmothèque. Their historical, cultural, and artistic significance is indispensable, and as can be seen, they are still alive and kicking!
